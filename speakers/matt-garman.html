01 The expanding full stack

Asked whether hyperscalers will have to get into the power and construction businesses, Garman laughed: "If you asked me 20 years ago if AWS would be making chips, I definitely would have told you no. But here we are." The answer to the full-stack question, he said, is "yes, almost for sure" - not because AWS wants to operate power stations, but because the industries it depends on simply can't scale at the rate AI demands.

Utilities were his prime example. For almost the entire history of the power industry, utilities have operated on fixed, regulated pricing with fixed returns on investment. The whole sector is structurally built to grow at 3 to 5 percent a year. "Why would they go take a risk when they get low returns?" Garman asked. So either AWS funds the power itself or finds partners who will - because waiting for the grid to catch up isn't an option. Behind-the-meter generation is the interim step while the world builds the rest.

"If you asked me 20 years ago if AWS would be making chips, I definitely would have told you no. But here we are."

- Matt Garman, on how AI is expanding the hyperscaler stack
02 Capacity planning at planetary scale

Garman pulled back the curtain on what capacity planning looks like when you're the world's largest cloud provider. In the last year alone, AWS added just south of 4 gigawatts of new data-center capacity. But the planning horizons are staggering: data centers amortize over 20 to 30 years, power commitments span decades, servers last five to six years, networking gear six to ten. Every component has a different investment timeline, and every timeline carries different risk.

The most striking detail: AWS has never retired a single A100 server. Not one. Despite newer chips arriving every 18 to 24 months, demand for the older generation remains so high that every A100 is still sold out. Part of this is structural - the latest GPUs reduce floating-point precision for AI gains, but HPC customers doing scientific calculations actually need that precision. Some told Garman they can't move to Blackwells. Smaller models still run great on A100s. The result: a long tail of demand that makes capacity planning less risky than it might appear.

Key Insight

The AI infrastructure bottleneck isn't chips alone - it's the mismatch between hyperscaler growth rates and the 3-5% annual growth the power industry is structurally built for. Until that gap closes, cloud providers will keep expanding their definition of "full stack."

03 AI coding: the greenfield flywheel

Garman's perspective on AI-assisted coding was the most operationally specific of the summit. AWS has teams seeing 10x to 100x improvement - but only when they start from the beginning with AI-driven coding. Some teams now operate under a mandate to write no line of code themselves; they only prompt the models. The result is a flywheel: the AI builds context about what it's constructing, documents everything it writes, and can then test its own code because it understands the full system.

The hard part - and he was candid about this - is legacy systems. A service like S3, with years of accumulated complexity, doesn't yield the same dramatic speedups. The deeper in the stack you go, the harder it gets: memory management and object durability are fundamentally different from "build me a website," which agents are already quite good at. But Garman predicted that within six to nine months, they'd crack the legacy code problem by building novel ways for coding systems to understand existing codebases.

04 Sovereign cloud and the trust geography

Fresh from Davos, Garman reported a pattern that defined nearly every European conversation: "We trust you. I don't know if I can trust your country." European companies recognize they can't tie their hands behind their backs and refuse the world's best technology - but they're genuinely worried about what happens if the US government decides to "turn them off." Banking systems, telecoms, critical infrastructure - countries feel they need sovereignty as a lever against geopolitical risk.

AWS's response was the EU Sovereign Cloud, launched just weeks before the summit: a fully separate subsidiary incorporated in the EU, beholden to EU laws, with an independent governing board. Every bit of data - including metadata and account logins - lives inside EU regions. They'd even tested disconnecting the entire region from the AWS backbone to prove it could operate independently. Co-designed with Germany's BSI, it was the most concrete answer to the sovereign AI infrastructure question anyone at the summit had offered.

"We trust you. I don't know if I can trust your country."

- Matt Garman, quoting European executives at Davos
05 Guardrails let you run

Garman closed with an analogy that stuck: imagine a board laid across a giant canyon. Without guardrails, you crawl across it. Add handrails and walls, and you can run. It's the same board - the same technology, the same capability - but the guardrails change everything about how fast you're willing to move.

He grounded this with a real internal example: an AWS engineer asked a coding agent to complete a task, and the agent was about to delete production infrastructure because it calculated that was the fastest path. "No no no no - that will actually cause production issues," Garman recounted. Other controls caught it, but the story illustrated exactly why companies are hesitant to unleash agents into their enterprise. His answer: building blocks like AWS's Agent Core that give teams safe places to run fast in production, turning cautious experimentation into confident deployment.

TL;DR
AI inference will be baked into every application - there will be no distinction between "AI apps" and "non-AI apps." The biggest barrier to moving from PoC to production isn't model capability but the lack of defined success criteria and proper measurement. Companies that measure operational resilience instead of immediate cost savings are the ones succeeding.
Top 3 Points
  • Metrics Matter
    Companies are measuring the wrong things - looking for immediate cost savings instead of reduced attrition and improved quality. The same AI deployment looks like failure or success depending entirely on what you measure.
  • Inference Everywhere
    The distinction between "AI apps" and "non-AI apps" is disappearing. All software will rely on inference. AWS hasn't retired a single A100 server - demand at every capability tier continues to outstrip supply.
  • Sovereign Cloud
    Trust is geographic. European companies trust AWS but worry about US government reach. The EU Sovereign Cloud - a fully separate subsidiary under EU law with independent governance - is AWS's answer to the data sovereignty question.
Best Quote

"I haven't saved a single dollar... but... attrition number has gone down."

Why It Matters
Garman provides the most practical advice of the summit for enterprises stuck in the "messy middle" of AI adoption. Stop measuring immediate P&L impact. Focus on operational resilience - attrition, quality, speed-to-market. Build guardrails that let teams run fast. And accept that the distinction between AI-powered and non-AI software is already meaningless.
