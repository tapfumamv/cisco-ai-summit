01 A tale of two adoption curves

Levie opened by framing enterprise AI adoption as a "barbell" - two extremes with little in between. On one side, the quick wins: deploying AI copilots across the organization, checking the box on broad access. On the other, the deep workflow reinvention that actually drives ROI - automating contract processes, re-engineering wealth management onboarding from a two-week process to a one-hour one, extracting upsell insights from unstructured customer data.

The problem, he argued, isn't the technology. It's that most companies are stuck in the shallow end, deploying copilots without rethinking the workflows those copilots are supposed to improve. Three to five genuine workflow wins over the next year or two, he said, would be the real differentiator.

02 The verifiability gap

Why has AI exploded in software development but stalled in the broader knowledge economy? Levie offered one of the session's sharpest insights: code is binary. It runs or it doesn't. You can test it, verify it, ship it. Legal documents, sales proposals, financial analyses? There's no equivalent "compile and run" button. The output is probabilistic, and enterprises don't have frameworks to verify it at scale.

This verifiability gap explains the CIO frustration he was clearly channeling. Companies are watching engineering teams achieve 10x productivity while their legal, HR, and sales teams see marginal improvements. The gap isn't in model capability - it's in the nature of the work itself.

"Instead of thinking agents will adapt to how we work, we will have to adapt to how agents work."

- Aaron Levie, on enterprise workflow reinvention
03 Adapt to how agents work

Here Levie delivered his central thesis - and it was a provocation aimed squarely at the CIOs in the room. The prevailing assumption is that AI agents will learn to navigate our existing processes. Levie flipped it: we will have to adapt to how agents work. Processes need to become machine-readable. Data needs to be structured for agent consumption. Workflows need to be re-engineered from the ground up, not papered over with a chatbot.

He illustrated this with an enterprise reality everyone recognized: you can't layer AI over broken analog processes and expect transformation. The contracts buried in file shares, the approval chains that exist only in someone's head, the unstructured memos that encode institutional knowledge - all of it needs to be made legible to agents before agents can act on it.

Key Insight

In a world with 1000x more agents than people in an enterprise - all probabilistic, all making the wrong decision some percentage of the time - the value of deterministic systems that traffic-cop what agents can access and do actually goes up, not down.

04 SaaS is not dead - it's evolving

When pressed on the "SaaS is dead" narrative popular on Twitter, Levie was characteristically direct: "You might imagine I'm going to say SaaS is not dead." But his defense was nuanced, not defensive. He conceded two things: software will be cheaper to build (more competition, lower prices) and agents will absorb some capabilities currently handled by software. Both inevitable.

What he rejected was the idea that enterprises would vibe-code their own ERP systems. Citing Geoffrey Moore's core versus context framework, he argued that building commodity software is never where you'd deploy scarce resources. And then he made the stronger case: in a world of a thousand agents per enterprise, all non-deterministic and probabilistic, the deterministic systems that govern what agents can access, what data they can modify, and what workflows they can execute become more valuable, not less. The system of record becomes the traffic cop in an agentic world.

05 Use agents to be more ambitious

Levie closed with a reframe that elevated the entire conversation. The instinct, he noted, is to use AI to do smaller things cheaper. The real unlock is using it to do bigger things faster. When agents lower the cost of experimentation by 10x, the ambitious projects that used to take three years to validate can be tested in two weeks. The ROI calculation flips entirely.

"Use agents to be more ambitious," he urged the CIO audience. Not just to cut costs, but to tackle the projects that were always too daunting to attempt - the ones that could actually transform a business. It was a fitting close from a CEO who has spent his career arguing that enterprise software should expand what's possible, not just optimize what already exists.

"Use agents to be more ambitious and to actually do way more as an organization - not just the smaller things, but actually the bigger things."

- Aaron Levie, on the real potential of AI
TL;DR
"Tale of Two Cities" - engineering adopts AI rapidly because code is verifiable (it runs or it doesn't), while general knowledge work lags because enterprise workflows are messy and lack binary verification. Companies must restructure work itself, not just layer AI on top.
Top 3 Points
  • Adapting to Agents
    Stop waiting for agents to adapt to our workflows - re-engineer processes to be machine-readable. You can't layer AI over broken analog processes and expect ROI.
  • Verifiability Gap
    Coding works because code runs or doesn't - binary verification. Legal, sales, and knowledge work lack this, explaining why AI ROI is lagging outside engineering.
  • Unstructured Data
    The biggest opportunity is unlocking value trapped in contracts, memos, and records - the unstructured enterprise data that agents need to be useful but can't yet access systematically.
