01 From Instagram to the AI frontier

Krieger traced a throughline from his Stanford days studying symbolic systems - "this very odd degree" combining cognitive science, design, philosophy, and AI - through co-founding Instagram, building Artifact (AI-powered news sold to Yahoo), and joining Anthropic. The key insight from Artifact was twofold: coding was about to change, and models would compress months of feature development into hours. He described drawing ASCII art of screens to feed into early coding models because they didn't have vision yet, going to make coffee while tokens generated at two per second - "but the trajectory was already there." It was clear to him that the next generation of software would be built and powered by labs.

02 Software is now a living, breathing system

Krieger challenged a widely held belief - that design is going away. His contrarian take: there's still a difference between software you use and software you love, and vibecoded products haven't closed that gap. But the bigger shift is in how product people must work. He described building computer use at Anthropic - prototyping Claude driving a computer in his third month, when it was "terrible, clicking all over the place." They kept the harness running, and with each new research model they'd test again. One day with Sonnet 3.7, a researcher pinged him: "It actually got good." The lesson: you need to get to the living, breathing system as quickly as possible.

"Software is now a living breathing system with this non-deterministic wonderful but also infuriating engine at its core."

- Mike Krieger, on the new product development reality
03 100% AI-written code is already here

When G2 asked "Is Claude being written by Claude?" - the answer was unequivocal: yes. For most products at Anthropic, it's effectively 100% AI-written code. The workflow has transformed around three key changes. First, Claude as adversarial code reviewer - trained to be a "super tough grader" that finds security vulnerabilities and refactoring opportunities before a human looks at it. Second, willingness to let Claude rearchitect entire systems when the initial approach hits a wall. Third - and most painful - every other process has become the bottleneck. When Anthropic's CI system went down, it wasn't a mild annoyance anymore; with engineers producing a dozen pull requests per day each, the outage was "physically painful."

Key Insight

The scarcity in software development has shifted from writing code to reviewing, auditing, integrating, and driving alignment on what the product should be - which was always the hard part, but now it's clearly seen as the hard part.

04 Don't be wedded to your form factor

Krieger shared a revealing failure-to-success story about spreadsheet integration. Anthropic tried four approaches: Claude reading spreadsheets and writing code to update them, Claude using computer vision to click within Excel ("one of the hardest problems you can give computer vision"), and two other iterations before landing on building hooks directly into Excel itself - "by far the most successful." His principle: don't be wedded to your form factor, be wedded to the problem you're solving. Your differentiator isn't your ability to write software - it's the trust, brand, customer relationships, and data you've built.

05 Enterprise adoption requires ambitious bets

Krieger drew a sharp line between enterprise AI approaches that work and those that fail. The piecemeal approach - building one custom agent for some low-value process - is "doomed to failure" because the problem isn't ambitious enough to learn from and people give up at the first roadblock. Instead, he advocated finding critical business processes where it "should feel a bit scary" that AI will handle them, then partnering closely on guardrails and timelines. The central AI team should be a thin facilitation layer - model access and observability - not a gatekeeper dictating which SDKs to use. Push decisions into product teams. Stay on the frontier.

TL;DR
We are moving toward software being 100% written by AI, which shifts the human role from writing syntax to architecting systems and reviewing "adversarial" code generated by models.
Top 3 Points
  • 100% AI Code
    Anthropic's internal products are effectively 100% written by Claude; the workflow has shifted to auditing and guiding the AI rather than typing code.
  • Adversarial Review
    A key workflow is asking the AI to be a "tough grader" on its own code to find security vulnerabilities and refactoring opportunities before a human even looks at it.
  • Form Factor Agility
    Product teams must not be wedded to specific interfaces (like trying to make AI use a spreadsheet visually) but must iterate until they find the right integration (hooks into Excel).
