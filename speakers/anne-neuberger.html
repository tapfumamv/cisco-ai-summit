01 AI becomes a weapon of war

Anne Neuberger didn't open with platitudes about AI's commercial potential. She opened with a stark reframing: AI is now a national security issue of the highest order. Not in the abstract, speculative sense that policy wonks have debated for years, but in the immediate, operational sense - AI is already shaping the outcomes of real conflicts. She drew directly from her role overseeing cyber and emerging technology policy, making clear that the conversations happening in boardrooms about AI adoption are also happening in situation rooms, with considerably higher stakes.

02 Machine-speed conflict

The most visceral moment came when Neuberger described the reality of machine-speed conflict. When missiles are inbound, she explained, human reaction time is simply too slow. The decision loop - detect, assess, decide, act - must happen in seconds, not minutes. "We are totally dependent right now on technology working," she said, her voice carrying the weight of someone who has sat in those rooms when the alerts go off. "If those missiles hit, you're going to have thousands of casualties." This wasn't hypothetical. This was operational reality, delivered to a room full of technologists who suddenly understood their work in a very different light.

"We are totally dependent right now on technology working... If those missiles hit, you're going to have thousands of casualties."

- Anne Neuberger, on machine-speed defense
03 The adversary moves faster

From defense, Neuberger pivoted to offense - specifically, how adversarial adoption of AI is outpacing the US. Criminals don't have procurement cycles. Hostile states don't have congressional oversight hearings. They don't debate safety frameworks or wait for NIST guidelines. They take the models, strip the guardrails, and deploy. She painted a picture of an asymmetric race where the side constrained by democratic norms and regulatory process is inherently slower to field new capabilities - and where that speed gap could be catastrophic.

04 Partnerships as force multiplier

Brett McGurk widened the aperture. His central thesis was deceptively simple: America's greatest advantage over its adversaries isn't technological - it's relational. The US has something China and Russia fundamentally lack: a global network of allies who want to cooperate. Dozens of nations with shared values, interoperable systems, and decades of trust built through treaty alliances and joint operations. Sharing advanced AI capabilities with allies, he argued, is not giving away the store - it's building collective strength that compounds. Each ally develops applications, surfaces edge cases, contributes data, and extends the reach of American-origin AI into domains and geographies the US cannot cover alone.

Key Insight

The AI race isn't just about building the best model. It's about whose values get embedded in the systems that will govern the world. Alliances aren't just a military strategy - they're a technology distribution network for democratic norms.

05 The technology cold war

McGurk didn't use the phrase "cold war" lightly, but his framing left little ambiguity. The US and China are engaged in a technology competition with civilizational stakes. It's not just about who builds the best model - it's about whose values get embedded in the AI systems that will increasingly govern commerce, education, media, and governance worldwide. Export controls on AI chips are a double-edged sword: they constrain adversaries in the short term but motivate China to build its own chip ecosystem. The policy, McGurk suggested, must be dynamic and adaptive, not a static wall. And the international security architecture - built for nuclear deterrence and conventional warfare - must be fundamentally updated for a world where the decisive strategic capability is software that improves itself every quarter.

06 The regulation paradox

The panel closed by naming the paradox that haunts every national security professional working on AI: the very regulation needed to ensure AI safety could slow the deployment needed to ensure national safety. It's not that regulation is wrong - it's that the adversary doesn't wait for your compliance framework. Neuberger and McGurk left the audience with an uncomfortable truth: AI is not just a commercial tool to be optimized for enterprise value. It is a munition in modern warfare, and the companies building it are, whether they like it or not, part of the defense industrial base of the twenty-first century.

TL;DR
AI has become a top-tier national security issue where machine-speed decision making in conflict necessitates the US maintaining its technological lead. The gap between adversarial adoption and defensive deployment is the most dangerous vulnerability in modern warfare, and America's network of alliances is the force multiplier that no amount of compute can replicate.
Top 3 Points
  • Machine-Speed Conflict
    Human reaction time is too slow for missile defense and cyberattacks. AI-driven decision making at machine speed is no longer optional - it's the difference between thousands of casualties and successful defense.
  • Partnerships as Strategy
    The US advantage over adversaries is its global alliance network - dozens of nations with shared values and interoperable systems. Technology sharing with allies builds collective strength that compounds and extends American reach.
  • Adversarial Speed
    Criminals and hostile states adopt AI faster than democracies because they aren't constrained by regulation, procurement cycles, or safety frameworks. The asymmetry is growing and could be catastrophic.
Best Quote

"We are totally dependent right now on technology working... If those missiles hit, you're going to have thousands of casualties."

Why It Matters
AI is not just a commercial tool but a munition in modern warfare and cybersecurity. The tension between the need for regulation and the need for deployment speed is the defining policy challenge - and every company building AI is now part of the defense industrial base whether they intended to be or not.
