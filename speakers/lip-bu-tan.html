01 The foundry grind

Where other speakers at the summit painted AI's future in sweeping strokes - trillion-dollar markets, civilizational transformation - Lip-Bu Tan brought the conversation crashing back to earth. Literally. AI runs on silicon, and silicon comes from factories where progress is measured in yield improvements, month by month. Tan's Intel is in the foundry business now, and he didn't sugarcoat what that means: it's a grind. You're investing in advanced packaging technologies - system-on-wafer architectures, coherent optics, glass substrates - and each step forward is won through painstaking engineering at the atomic scale. There are no software-style overnight breakthroughs when you're working with the periodic table. Tan's tone was that of an engineer-CEO who finds this challenge invigorating rather than discouraging: "I continue to have that curiosity," he said, describing how he's personally exploring new materials from gallium nitride to artificial diamond.

02 Memory is the real bottleneck

The summit's most counter-narrative insight came when Tan shifted the conversation from GPUs - the component everyone obsesses over - to memory. "If anything is going to slow down AI," he said plainly, "it's going to be the memory." High-bandwidth memory (HBM) is the critical constraint, and there's no relief expected until 2028. Every AI accelerator, no matter how powerful, is ultimately throttled by how fast it can feed data to its compute cores. This was a reality check aimed squarely at the audience of enterprise leaders and investors who may have assumed that GPU supply was the only bottleneck worth watching. Tan was saying: even if you solve the GPU problem, you hit the memory wall next. And that wall is at least two years away from giving way.

"If anything is going to slow down AI, it's going to be the memory."

03 Not just CPUs - GPUs, new materials, new architectures

Perhaps the most revealing moment of Tan's appearance was how openly he broke from Intel's historical identity. He announced that he'd hired a new chief GPU architect, that Intel would build GPUs alongside CPUs, that the company would embrace RISC-V and ARM rather than clinging to x86 alone, and that "Software 2.0" means letting software requirements drive hardware design rather than the reverse. He rattled off explorations in glass substrates, artificial diamond for thermal management, gallium nitride for RF - walking the audience through the periodic table of materials science as though it were a startup pitch deck. The message was unmistakable: Intel under Tan is no longer a CPU company. It's a materials and manufacturing company that happens to make chips - and it will make whatever chips the AI era demands.

Key Insight

The AI hardware bottleneck that matters most isn't the one everyone's watching. While the industry obsesses over GPU supply, high-bandwidth memory (HBM) is the binding constraint - and it won't be resolved until 2028. Physical supply chains, not software innovation, may determine the pace of AI progress.

04 China's adaptation under pressure

Tan addressed the geopolitical dimension with the directness of someone who has spent decades investing in semiconductor companies across Asia. Despite U.S. export controls designed to limit China's access to cutting-edge chip technology, Chinese companies are not standing still. They're aggressively recruiting talent, optimizing their architectures to extract maximum performance from available technology, and in some cases finding creative paths to leapfrog constraints entirely. Tan didn't frame this as a threat to be feared - he framed it as competitive pressure to be respected. For the enterprise audience, the implication was clear: the global AI race is not just about who has the best models. It's about who controls the physical supply chain - and that race is far from settled.

05 Advice to enterprises: start from the foundation

When asked directly what advice he'd give to the CIOs and CISOs in the room, Tan was characteristically practical. "Think about what is the problem you're trying to solve. What is the outcome you want to look for." He shared that at Intel itself, he'd recruited "the best CIO I can find" and told her to look at the foundation - not just layer AI on top of legacy infrastructure. "You don't just put on top of the old legacy. It's not going to work." He then cited an MIT professor's research showing that despite all the AI hype, global economic productivity growth remains stubbornly low - a data point that visibly shocked him. The lesson: AI's promise is real, but enterprises must rebuild their foundations, measure actual productivity gains, and present evidence to their boards. There's no shortcut from silicon to strategy - and Tan, more than anyone at the summit, embodied that truth.

TL;DR
Manufacturing capacity and memory (not just logic chips) are critical bottlenecks for AI scaling, with a fierce race to optimize infrastructure at every level of the physical stack.
