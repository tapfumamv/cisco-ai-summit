01 Here be lions

Katsoudas opened with a deceptively simple question: who in your organization is using AI most effectively? When she asked Cisco's leaders to name their power users, the results were startling - the names weren't the loudest voices or the most senior people, and many weren't even marked as critical to retain. The talent systems were blind to them. This led her to a powerful historical metaphor: medieval cartographers who couldn't describe the unknown didn't leave maps blank - they wrote "here be lions" at the edges. The map didn't end at the boundary of knowledge. It ended at the boundary of risk. She argued today's lions are ambiguity, leadership gaps, and ethical uncertainty - and they explain why only 13% of companies feel they're truly getting value from AI.

"The map didn't end at the boundary of knowledge. It ended at the boundary of risk."

- Fran Katsoudas, on why organizations stall at AI adoption
02 The activated employee

Katsoudas introduced Cisco's framework for the "activated employee" - defined as performance plus engagement plus self-motivation. The data was striking: these employees adopted AI twice as fast as their peers. They leaned into learning aggressively. And despite being the most marketable people in the company, they had higher retention rates than average and scored higher in pride for the Cisco brand. She also shared that AI adoption cuts across demographics in surprising ways - both brand-new hires and 20-year veterans were leading the charge. She illustrated this with "Jordan," a cyber analyst who went from manually reviewing incident reports and playing whack-a-mole to running AI-generated triage and attack simulations. The point wasn't abstract: telling concrete "skill stories" like Jordan's reduces fear and makes the transformation tangible.

Key Insight

Your most valuable AI adopters may be invisible to your existing talent systems. Both new hires and 20-year veterans are leading AI adoption - it's not a generational story, it's an agency story.

03 The leadership multiplier

Perhaps the most actionable finding: non-AI users remain non-users unless their leader intervenes. Katsoudas was blunt - adoption doesn't follow an email. "Surprise." It follows the leader. When a leader personally uses AI, their team's adoption doesn't just grow - it doubles. She addressed the room directly: "You are the 2x multiplier." She also pushed back on the narrative that the C-suite is blaming the workforce for slow AI transitions, referencing a woman at Davos who challenged her on this perception. Katsoudas argued we need to flip the script - stop talking about skill gaps and resistance, start expressing confidence in the workforce and spotlighting employees who are already innovating.

"When a leader uses AI, their team adoption doesn't just grow. It doubles. You are the 2x multiplier."

- Fran Katsoudas, on the leadership effect
04 The efficiency paradox

Katsoudas surfaced a concerning finding she called the efficiency paradox: the most active AI users sometimes reported lower levels of team trust. While AI makes individuals move at light speed, it can fracture the team dynamic. Her framing was careful - "AI isn't breaking teams. It's stress-testing them." The old ways of building trust have to evolve. At Cisco, the belief is that AI is a team sport, and getting it right means elevating what's deeply human about work rather than losing connection. Through their AI Workforce Consortium with nine companies, they found that collaborating across company walls produced greater trust in findings, more clarity for employees, and acceleration for everyone.

05 Walking with the lions

Katsoudas closed with a call to collective responsibility. She announced the consortium's report - covering 200+ free learning courses from nine companies, a workforce architecture playbook, a skills glossary, and "skill stories" showing how roles evolve with AI. She noted that 78% of technology roles now require AI skills: "The shift isn't coming. The shift is already here." She extended the lens beyond the Fortune 500, arguing that if AI's lions are paralyzing for large enterprises, imagine their effect on communities worldwide. This connected to Cisco's eight-year Global Citizen partnership (43 million actions, 1.3 billion lives impacted, $49 billion distributed). Her closing line brought the metaphor full circle: "The future does not belong to those who wait for the map to be finished. It belongs to those who fearlessly walk with the lions."

TL;DR
The workforce is split between those paralyzed by ambiguity ("here be lions") and "activated" employees who use AI to perform 2x better; the leader's personal AI usage is the single biggest factor in team adoption.
Top 3 Points

    Cisco's data shows activated employees - those with high performance, engagement, and self-motivation - adopt AI twice as fast, have higher retention, and score higher in brand pride. They exist across all demographics, from new hires to 20-year veterans.
  • The Leadership Multiplier
    Non-AI users stay non-users unless their leader intervenes. When a leader personally uses AI, team adoption doubles. Adoption follows behavior, not emails or mandates.
  • The Efficiency Paradox
    The most active AI users sometimes report lower team trust. AI makes individuals faster but can fracture team dynamics - the old models of trust-building need to evolve for an AI-augmented workplace.
Best Quote

"The map didn't end at the boundary of knowledge. It ended at the boundary of risk."

Why It Matters
Katsoudas provides the human resources counter-narrative: AI doesn't just require technical training; it requires a culture shift where leaders actively model usage. The data is clear - leadership behavior is the single biggest lever for AI adoption, and the companies that figure out how to maintain trust while accelerating will win.
